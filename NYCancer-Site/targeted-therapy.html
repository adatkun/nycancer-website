Targeted Therapy

Precision cancer treatment that targets specific genetic changes in cancer cells.

Targeted therapy represents a significant advancement in precision cancer medicine. At New York Cancer & Blood Specialists, we offer cutting-edge targeted treatments that precisely attack cancer cells based on their specific genetic alterations, often with fewer side effects than traditional chemotherapy.

What is Targeted Therapy?

Targeted therapy is a type of cancer treatment that precisely identifies and attacks specific types of cancer cells while causing less harm to normal cells. Unlike traditional chemotherapy, which attacks all rapidly dividing cells, targeted therapies interfere with specific molecules involved in cancer growth, progression, and spread.

These treatments work by targeting:

  • Specific genetic mutations that drive cancer growth
  • Proteins that are overexpressed on cancer cells
  • Signaling pathways that cancer cells use to grow and survive
  • The tumor microenvironment that supports cancer development
  • Blood vessel formation that supplies tumors with nutrients

By focusing on these specific targets, these medications can block cancer growth and spread while typically causing fewer side effects than conventional chemotherapy. Targeted therapy has revolutionized cancer treatment by enabling more personalized approaches based on the unique characteristics of each patient's cancer.

Types of Targeted Therapy

New York Cancer & Blood Specialists offers a comprehensive range of targeted therapies, addressing various cellular mechanisms that drive cancer growth:

Small Molecule Inhibitors

These drugs are small enough to enter cancer cells and interfere with specific molecules involved in tumor growth and spread. Major categories include:

  • Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors (TKIs) - Block enzymes called tyrosine kinases that are crucial for cell signaling pathways
    • EGFR inhibitors (e.g., osimertinib, erlotinib) for certain lung cancers
    • BCR-ABL inhibitors (e.g., imatinib, dasatinib) for chronic myeloid leukemia
    • BRAF inhibitors (e.g., vemurafenib, dabrafenib) for melanoma and other cancers with BRAF mutations
    • HER2 inhibitors (e.g., tucatinib, neratinib) for HER2-positive breast cancer
    • ALK inhibitors (e.g., alectinib, lorlatinib) for ALK-positive lung cancer
    • BTK inhibitors (e.g., ibrutinib, acalabrutinib) for certain blood cancers
  • mTOR inhibitors (e.g., everolimus, temsirolimus) - Block a protein that regulates cell growth and survival
  • PARP inhibitors (e.g., olaparib, niraparib) - Prevent cancer cells from repairing their DNA, leading to cell death
  • CDK4/6 inhibitors (e.g., palbociclib, ribociclib) - Block proteins involved in cell division
  • Proteasome inhibitors (e.g., bortezomib, carfilzomib) - Prevent the breakdown of proteins that control cell growth
  • IDH inhibitors (e.g., ivosidenib, enasidenib) - Target mutations in isocitrate dehydrogenase in certain leukemias

Monoclonal Antibodies

These laboratory-made proteins are designed to bind to specific targets on cancer cells. They work in several ways:

  • Blocking growth signals
    • HER2 antibodies (e.g., trastuzumab) for HER2-positive breast and gastric cancers
    • EGFR antibodies (e.g., cetuximab) for colorectal and head and neck cancers
  • Delivering toxic substances to cancer cells (Antibody-drug conjugates)
    • T-DM1 (trastuzumab emtansine) for HER2-positive breast cancer
    • Enfortumab vedotin for bladder cancer
    • Sacituzumab govitecan for triple-negative breast cancer
  • Marking cancer cells for immune system attack

Angiogenesis Inhibitors

These drugs block the formation of new blood vessels that tumors need to grow:

  • VEGF inhibitors (e.g., bevacizumab) for colorectal, lung, kidney, and other cancers
  • Multi-targeted TKIs with anti-angiogenic properties (e.g., sunitinib, pazopanib) for kidney cancer and others

Hormone Therapies

For cancers that are driven by hormones, these targeted approaches either reduce hormone levels or block hormone effects:

  • For breast cancer: Selective estrogen receptor modulators (e.g., tamoxifen), aromatase inhibitors (e.g., letrozole), and selective estrogen receptor degraders (e.g., fulvestrant)
  • For prostate cancer: Anti-androgens (e.g., enzalutamide, apalutamide), androgen synthesis inhibitors (e.g., abiraterone)

Immunomodulatory Drugs

These medications adjust immune responses and affect the tumor microenvironment:

  • Thalidomide, lenalidomide, and pomalidomide for multiple myeloma and certain lymphomas

Molecular Testing and Precision Medicine

Effective targeted therapy depends on identifying the specific genetic changes driving an individual's cancer. At New York Cancer & Blood Specialists, we offer comprehensive molecular testing to match patients with the most appropriate targeted treatments:

Advanced Diagnostic Technologies

  • Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) - Allows simultaneous analysis of hundreds of genes to identify mutations, fusions, and other alterations
  • Liquid Biopsies - Blood tests that can detect circulating tumor DNA, providing genetic information without an invasive tissue biopsy
  • Immunohistochemistry (IHC) - Detects specific proteins that may be targets for therapy
  • Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH) - Identifies specific genetic changes such as translocations and amplifications
  • RNA Sequencing - Examines gene expression patterns in cancer cells
  • Proteomics - Studies the proteins produced by cancer cells

Key Biomarkers for Targeted Therapy

Depending on your cancer type, we may test for specific biomarkers including:

  • EGFR, ALK, ROS1, BRAF, NTRK, MET, RET mutations and fusions in lung cancer
  • HER2 amplification or mutation in breast, gastric, and other cancers
  • BRAF, KRAS, NRAS mutations in colorectal and other cancers
  • BRCA1/2 mutations in ovarian, breast, pancreatic, and prostate cancers
  • PIK3CA mutations in breast cancer
  • FGFR alterations in bladder and other cancers
  • IDH1/2 mutations in leukemia and glioma
  • BCR-ABL fusion in chronic myeloid leukemia
  • Hormone receptor status (estrogen and progesterone receptors) in breast cancer

Targeted Therapy for Different Cancer Types

Many cancer types can be treated with targeted therapies. Here are some examples of how targeted therapy is used for various cancers:

Lung Cancer

Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) has seen remarkable advances in targeted therapy:

  • EGFR inhibitors for EGFR-mutated NSCLC
  • ALK inhibitors for ALK-positive NSCLC
  • ROS1 inhibitors for ROS1-positive NSCLC
  • BRAF/MEK inhibitor combinations for BRAF V600E-mutated NSCLC
  • MET, RET, and NTRK targeted therapies for cancers with these alterations
  • KRAS G12C inhibitors for NSCLC with this specific mutation

Breast Cancer

Several targeted approaches are used for breast cancer:

  • HER2-targeted antibodies and small molecules for HER2-positive breast cancer
  • CDK4/6 inhibitors combined with hormone therapy for HR-positive/HER2-negative breast cancer
  • PI3K inhibitors for PIK3CA-mutated breast cancer
  • PARP inhibitors for BRCA-mutated breast cancer
  • Antibody-drug conjugates for HER2-low and triple-negative breast cancer

Colorectal Cancer

Targeted therapy options include:

  • EGFR inhibitors for RAS wild-type metastatic colorectal cancer
  • VEGF inhibitors to block blood vessel formation
  • BRAF/MEK/EGFR inhibitor combinations for BRAF V600E-mutated colorectal cancer
  • HER2-targeted therapy for HER2-amplified colorectal cancer

Blood Cancers

Leukemias, lymphomas, and myeloma have seen dramatic improvements with targeted therapies:

  • BCR-ABL inhibitors for chronic myeloid leukemia
  • BTK inhibitors for chronic lymphocytic leukemia and certain lymphomas
  • BCL-2 inhibitors for certain leukemias and lymphomas
  • IDH inhibitors for acute myeloid leukemia with IDH mutations
  • Proteasome inhibitors and immunomodulatory drugs for multiple myeloma
  • CD20-targeted antibodies for B-cell lymphomas

Other Cancer Types

Targeted therapy continues to expand to many other cancers:

  • BRAF/MEK inhibitors for melanoma with BRAF mutations
  • VEGF and mTOR inhibitors for renal cell carcinoma
  • PARP inhibitors for ovarian, pancreatic, and prostate cancers with BRCA mutations
  • FGFR inhibitors for bladder cancer with FGFR alterations
  • Androgen receptor pathway inhibitors for prostate cancer
  • NTRK inhibitors for any cancer with NTRK gene fusions

Advantages and Considerations of Targeted Therapy

Potential Advantages

  • Precision - Attacks cancer cells more specifically than traditional chemotherapy
  • Effectiveness - Can produce dramatic and rapid responses in cancers with specific targets
  • Reduced side effects - Often causes fewer and different side effects than traditional chemotherapy
  • Oral administration - Many targeted therapies are pills taken at home rather than intravenous treatments
