Understanding Breast Cancer

Breast cancer occurs when cells in breast tissue grow uncontrollably. Understanding your risk factors and the basics empowers you to take proactive steps for your health.

1 in 8 women will be diagnosed with breast cancer in their lifetime

In the US: About 287,000 women will be diagnosed this year

Key Facts

  • Most common cancer in women after skin cancer
  • Can also occur in men, though rarely (about 1% of cases)
  • Not all breast lumps are cancerous - most are benign
  • Family history accounts for only 5-10% of cases
  • Risk increases with age, but can occur at any age

Types of Breast Cancer

  • Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) - Cancer cells are found in the lining of a duct but have not spread outside the duct
  • Invasive ductal carcinoma - The most common type of breast cancer, which starts in the milk ducts and spreads to surrounding breast tissue
  • Invasive lobular carcinoma - Cancer that begins in the lobules (milk-producing glands) and spreads to surrounding tissue
  • Inflammatory breast cancer - A rare but aggressive form of breast cancer
  • Triple-negative breast cancer - Cancer cells that lack estrogen receptors, progesterone receptors, and HER2 proteins
  • HER2-positive breast cancer - Cancer cells with high levels of HER2 protein

When to See a Doctor
Recognizing signs and taking action
While regular screening is essential, it's important to be aware of changes in your breasts and know when to contact your healthcare provider immediately.

Warning Signs

  • New lump or mass in breast or underarm area
  • Change in size or shape of breast
  • Dimpling or puckering of breast skin
  • Nipple turning inward or discharge (especially bloody)
  • Redness, warmth, or swelling of breast
  • Persistent breast or nipple pain

Risk Factors

  • Family history of breast or ovarian cancer
  • Personal history of breast or ovarian cancer
  • BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutations
  • Previous radiation therapy to chest area
  • Taking hormone replacement therapy
Remember

Trust your instincts. If something feels different or concerning, don't hesitate to contact your healthcare provider.

Benefits of Early Detection
Why it makes all the difference
Early detection opens doors to more treatment options and better outcomes. When found early, you have the greatest number of choices and the best chance for successful treatment.

Treatment Advantages

  • More treatment options to choose from
  • Less invasive surgical procedures available
  • Greater chance of breast-conserving surgery
  • Potentially shorter treatment duration
  • Better preservation of normal activities during treatment

Quality of Life Benefits

  • Maintain your normal routine more easily
  • Faster return to activities you enjoy
  • Less disruption to family and work commitments
  • Greater peace of mind knowing you caught it early
  • More energy and strength throughout treatment
Remember

Early detection empowers you with choices. The sooner we find it, the more options you have for treatment that fits your life.

Screening Guidelines
Your roadmap to effective screening
Regular breast cancer screening is one of the most important steps you can take for your health. Our comprehensive program uses advanced technology to detect changes before you can feel them.

Age Guidelines

  • Ages 40-44: Annual mammograms recommended (discuss with your doctor)
  • Ages 45-54: Annual mammograms strongly recommended
  • Ages 55+: Mammograms every 1-2 years, or continue annually
  • High-risk individuals: May start screening earlier with additional methods

Screening Methods

  • 3D Mammography (Tomosynthesis) - our gold standard
  • Breast MRI for high-risk patients
  • Breast ultrasound as supplemental screening
  • Clinical breast examination during regular check-ups
  • Genetic counseling and testing when appropriate
