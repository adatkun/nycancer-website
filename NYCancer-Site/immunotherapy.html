Immunotherapy

Advanced cancer treatment that harnesses the power of your own immune system to fight cancer.

Immunotherapy represents one of the most significant advances in cancer treatment in recent decades. At New York Cancer & Blood Specialists, we offer cutting-edge immunotherapy treatments that help your own immune system recognize and attack cancer cells more effectively, providing new hope for patients with many types of cancer.

What is Immunotherapy?

Immunotherapy is a form of cancer treatment that works by helping your body's immune system fight cancer. Unlike chemotherapy, which directly attacks cancer cells, immunotherapy enhances your immune system's natural ability to recognize and destroy cancer cells.

The immune system normally identifies and eliminates abnormal cells, including cancer cells. However, cancer cells can develop ways to evade or suppress immune responses. Immunotherapy helps overcome these immune evasion strategies, allowing your immune system to recognize cancer cells as threats and mount an effective response against them.

Immunotherapy has revolutionized cancer care by:

  • Providing treatment options for previously untreatable cancers
  • Delivering durable responses that can last for years after treatment ends
  • Offering new hope for patients with advanced or recurrent cancers
  • Often causing fewer side effects than traditional chemotherapy
  • Working effectively in combination with other cancer treatments

Types of Immunotherapy

New York Cancer & Blood Specialists offers several types of immunotherapy, each working differently to enhance immune responses against cancer:

Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors

Checkpoint inhibitors work by blocking proteins that prevent immune cells from attacking cancer. By removing these "brakes" on the immune system, these drugs help T cells recognize and attack cancer cells more effectively. FDA-approved checkpoint inhibitors include:

  • PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors (e.g., pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab, durvalumab)
  • CTLA-4 inhibitors (e.g., ipilimumab)
  • LAG-3 inhibitors (e.g., relatlimab)

These medications have shown remarkable success in treating melanoma, lung cancer, bladder cancer, kidney cancer, Hodgkin lymphoma, and many other cancer types.

Monoclonal Antibodies

These laboratory-made proteins are designed to target specific antigens on cancer cells. Some act directly by binding to cancer cells and marking them for destruction by the immune system, while others block growth signals or deliver drugs directly to cancer cells. Examples include:

  • HER2-targeted antibodies for breast and gastric cancers (e.g., trastuzumab)
  • EGFR-targeted antibodies for colorectal and head and neck cancers (e.g., cetuximab)
  • CD20-targeted antibodies for certain lymphomas and leukemias (e.g., rituximab)
  • Antibody-drug conjugates that deliver chemotherapy directly to cancer cells

Cytokines

Cytokines are proteins that help control immune cell growth and activity. Synthetic versions can boost overall immune responses against cancer:

  • Interleukins that help immune cells grow and divide
  • Interferons that help the immune system fight cancer and may slow tumor growth

CAR T-Cell Therapy

Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy is a form of cellular immunotherapy in which a patient's own T cells are collected, genetically modified to better recognize cancer cells, and then reinfused. This groundbreaking approach has shown remarkable success in treating certain blood cancers, including:

  • Certain types of leukemia
  • Some forms of lymphoma
  • Multiple myeloma

Cancer Vaccines

Unlike preventive vaccines, treatment vaccines work by teaching the immune system to recognize and attack specific cancer-related antigens. Currently approved cancer vaccines include:

  • Sipuleucel-T for advanced prostate cancer
  • Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) for bladder cancer

Many other cancer vaccines are being studied in clinical trials for various cancer types.

Oncolytic Virus Therapy

This innovative approach uses modified viruses that infect and kill cancer cells while sparing normal cells. As the viruses replicate and cause cancer cells to rupture, they also trigger an immune response against the cancer.

Cancers Treated with Immunotherapy

Immunotherapy has been approved for the treatment of numerous cancer types, and ongoing research continues to expand its applications. Cancer types that may respond to immunotherapy include:

  • Melanoma
  • Non-small cell lung cancer
  • Small cell lung cancer
  • Kidney cancer
  • Bladder cancer
  • Head and neck cancers
  • Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Non-Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Liver cancer
  • Colorectal cancer (with specific genetic features)
  • Breast cancer (certain types)
  • Cervical cancer
  • Esophageal cancer
  • Gastric cancer
  • Prostate cancer
  • Certain leukemias
  • Multiple myeloma
  • Other rare cancers

Additionally, certain immunotherapies have been approved for treating cancers with specific genetic features regardless of where in the body the cancer started, an approach known as "tumor-agnostic" treatment.

Biomarker Testing for Immunotherapy

Not all patients will respond to immunotherapy, which is why biomarker testing is crucial for identifying those most likely to benefit. Important biomarkers include:

  • PD-L1 expression - Higher levels may predict better response to checkpoint inhibitors
  • Tumor Mutational Burden (TMB) - Higher numbers of mutations may indicate better response
  • Microsatellite Instability (MSI) or Mismatch Repair Deficiency (dMMR) - Genetic features that often respond well to immunotherapy
  • Specific genetic alterations that may predict response or resistance

At New York Cancer & Blood Specialists, we perform comprehensive biomarker testing to determine if immunotherapy is right for you and to select the most appropriate treatment approach.

The Immunotherapy Treatment Process

When you come to New York Cancer & Blood Specialists for immunotherapy, you can expect a thorough, personalized approach to your care:

Evaluation and Preparation

  • Comprehensive assessment of your cancer type, stage, and prior treatments
  • Biomarker testing to determine if immunotherapy is appropriate
  • Detailed discussion of potential benefits, risks, and side effects
  • Baseline testing including blood work, imaging, and evaluation of organ function
  • Education about what to expect and how to manage potential side effects

Treatment Administration

  • Comfortable infusion centers with private and semi-private settings
  • Treatment schedules that vary depending on the specific immunotherapy:
    • Every 2-6 weeks for most checkpoint inhibitors
    • More frequent administration for some monoclonal antibodies
    • One-time treatment for certain cellular therapies
  • Experienced nursing staff specialized in administering immunotherapy
  • Monitoring for immediate infusion reactions

Ongoing Monitoring

  • Regular follow-up appointments to assess treatment response
  • Periodic imaging studies to evaluate tumor size and spread
  • Blood tests to monitor organ function and immune status
  • Side effect management – immunotherapy side effects can be very different from chemotherapy side effects and may require specialized management
  • Treatment adjustments as needed based on response and tolerability

Long-term Follow-up

  • Ongoing surveillance after completing treatment
  • Monitoring for delayed immune-related side effects, which can occur even after treatment ends
  • Survivorship care planning
  • Assessment for potential maintenance therapy when appropriate

Advantages and Considerations of Immunotherapy

Potential Advantages

  • Durable responses - Immunotherapy can lead to long-lasting remissions, sometimes continuing even after treatment stops
  • Effectiveness in advanced cancers where other treatments have failed
  • Different side effect profile than chemotherapy, often with less hair loss, nausea, and bone marrow suppression
  • Potential for "memory" response - The immune system may remember cancer cells, providing ongoing protection
  • Can work well in combination with other treatment approaches

Important Considerations

  • Not effective for all patients - Response rates vary depending on cancer type and individual factors
  • Unique side effects - Immune-related adverse events can affect any organ system and require prompt management
  • Response patterns differ from conventional treatments - tumors may initially increase in size before shrinking ("pseudoprogression")
  • Timing of response - May take longer to see effects compared to other treatments
  • Cost considerations - Immunotherapy can be expensive, though financial assistance may be available

Managing Immune-Related Side Effects

While immunotherapy often has fewer side effects than chemotherapy, it can cause unique immune-related adverse events (irAEs) as the activated immune system sometimes affects healthy tissues. Our team has extensive experience in recognizing and managing these side effects, which may include:

  • Skin reactions - Rash, itching, vitiligo
  • Gastrointestinal issues - Diarrhea, colitis
  • Endocrine problems - Thyroid disorders, diabetes, adrenal insufficiency
  • Lung inflammation - Pneumonitis
  • Liver inflammation - Hepatitis
  • Less common effects on other organs including heart, kidneys, and nervous system

Early recognition and management of these side effects is crucial, so we provide comprehensive education and close monitoring throughout your treatment.

Immunotherapy Research and Innovation

Immunotherapy is one of the most rapidly evolving areas in cancer treatment. At New York Cancer & Blood Specialists, we are committed to bringing cutting-edge immunotherapy approaches to our patients through:

Clinical Trials

We maintain an active clinical trials program investigating:

  • Novel immunotherapy agents
  • Innovative combination approaches
  • Immunotherapy for cancer types not currently approved for standard treatment
  • Strategies to overcome resistance to immunotherapy
  • Methods to reduce side effects while maintaining efficacy

Emerging Immunotherapy Approaches

Our oncologists stay at the forefront of immunotherapy research, evaluating promising new approaches such as:

  • Bispecific antibodies that can bind to cancer cells and immune cells simultaneously
  • Tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy using immune cells found within tumors
  • Natural killer (NK) cell therapies
  • Personalized cancer vaccines tailored to a patient's specific tumor mutations
  • Combinations of different immunotherapy types to enhance effectiveness
  • Neoadjuvant immunotherapy given before surgery

Precision Immunotherapy

We are working toward increasingly personalized immunotherapy approaches by:

  • Utilizing comprehensive genomic profiling to identify optimal candidates
  • Exploring novel biomarkers that predict response
  • Developing strategies to monitor immune responses in real-time
  • Investigating the role of the microbiome in immunotherapy outcomes
