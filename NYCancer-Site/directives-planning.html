Understanding Advance Directives

Advance directives are legal documents that outline your medical care wishes if you are no longer able to make your own decisions. These documents serve as your voice when you cannot speak for yourself, providing clear guidance to your healthcare team and loved ones about your treatment preferences.

Having advance directives in place is not about giving up hope or expecting the worstâ€”it's about taking control of your healthcare journey and ensuring your values and preferences are honored in all circumstances. It also relieves your loved ones from having to make difficult decisions without knowing your wishes.

The Purpose of Advance Directives

Advance directives serve two critical purposes in your healthcare journey:

Respecting Your Healthcare Preferences

Advance directives ensure that your specific healthcare wishes are respected when you are unable to communicate them yourself. This includes preferences about life-sustaining treatments, pain management, and other medical interventions. By documenting these preferences in advance, you maintain control over your healthcare decisions even when you cannot actively participate in the decision-making process.

Guiding Healthcare Decisions

These documents provide essential guidance to healthcare providers and family members who may need to make medical decisions on your behalf. Clear advance directives can prevent confusion, conflict, and unnecessary distress among your loved ones during already challenging times. They also give your healthcare team the confidence that they are providing care that aligns with your values and wishes.

Understanding Each Directive Type

Health Care Proxy (HCP)

A health care proxy is a legal document that appoints someone to make medical decisions for you if you are unable to do so yourself, such as due to incapacitation or illness. This appointed individual, known as your health care agent or proxy, can make choices regarding your medical treatment, including decisions about life-sustaining treatments and procedures.

Choosing Your Health Care Agent

When selecting a health care agent, consider someone who:

  • Understands your values and preferences
  • Is willing to advocate for your wishes, even if they differ from their own
  • Can make difficult decisions during stressful situations
  • Is likely to be available if and when needed
  • Can communicate effectively with healthcare providers

Living Will

A living will, also known as an advanced healthcare directive, is a legal document that outlines your wishes regarding medical treatment, particularly end-of-life care, if you become unable to make decisions for yourself. It's a way to express your preferences for treatments like CPR, mechanical ventilation, and other life-sustaining interventions.

What to Include in Your Living Will

Your living will should address your preferences regarding:

  • Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR)
  • Mechanical ventilation
  • Tube feeding
  • Dialysis
  • Antibiotics and antiviral medications
  • Comfort care preferences
  • Organ and tissue donation

Guardianship

Guardianship is a legal process where a court appoints a person or entity to make decisions for another person who cannot make decisions for themselves. Unlike a health care proxy, which you choose yourself in advance, guardianship is established through court proceedings when someone is already incapacitated and has no advance directives in place.

Surrogate

A "surrogate" is a person who acts as a substitute for another, whether in legal, medical, or other contexts. In healthcare, a surrogate decision-maker is someone who makes healthcare decisions for a person who cannot make or communicate their own decisions. Some states have surrogate decision-making laws that establish a priority list of who can serve as a surrogate if no health care proxy has been designated.

Our Approach to Advance Directive Planning

At New York Cancer & Blood Specialists, we integrate advance directive planning into our comprehensive patient care. As part of the Oncology Medical Home (OMH) model we follow, goals of care and advanced care planning discussions are a core standard of our practice.

Personalized Discussions

Your care team will schedule time to hold a "goals of care" discussion with you and your loved ones. These conversations address important life and care decisions to facilitate the implementation of your personal wishes. These discussions are never rushed and are tailored to your specific situation, values, and concerns.

Ongoing Support

We recognize that your preferences may evolve over time as your health situation changes. Our team provides ongoing support for reviewing and updating your advance directives to ensure they continue to reflect your current wishes.

Coordination with Your Care Team

Your advance directives become an integral part of your comprehensive care plan. With your permission, we ensure that all members of your healthcare team, both within our practice and at other facilities, are aware of your directives and have access to these important documents.

Getting Started with Advance Directive Planning

Creating advance directives is a thoughtful process that deserves careful consideration. Our team is here to support you every step of the way.

Step 1: Reflection and Discussion

Begin by reflecting on your values, preferences, and what matters most to you regarding your healthcare. Discuss these thoughts with trusted family members, friends, and your healthcare providers to gain clarity and perspective.

Step 2: Document Preparation

Our team can provide you with the necessary forms and resources to create legally valid advance directives. While these documents don't typically require a lawyer, they usually need to be witnessed or notarized to be legally binding.

Step 3: Communication and Distribution

Once your advance directives are completed, share copies with your healthcare proxy, family members, and all your healthcare providers. We will include these documents in your medical record with your permission.

Step 4: Regular Review

Plan to review your advance directives periodically, especially after significant health changes or life events. Your care team will remind you about the importance of keeping these documents current.
