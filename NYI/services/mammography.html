Mammography

Low-dose breast imaging used to help detect breast cancer early, with a patient-centered experience focused on comfort, clarity, and care.

What Is a Mammogram?

Regular mammograms are tests used by doctors to detect breast cancer early, and it can be detected up to three years before it can be felt. A mammogram is a low dose X-ray picture of the breast. These pictures allow doctors to see the internal parts of the breast to detect cancer.

Important Note

A trained technologist performs your exam, but they cannot provide results. A radiologist reads your images and reports the findings to you and your doctor.

How a Mammogram Is Done

Your mammogram is quick, and each image takes only a few moments.

1

Positioning

You will stand in front of a special X-ray machine while a trained technologist positions you for imaging.

2

Compression

Your breast is placed on a plastic plate and another plate presses firmly from above to flatten the breast and hold it still during the X-ray.

3

Views

These steps are repeated to create standard views. The other breast is imaged the same way.

4

Image Check

You will wait briefly while the technologist checks the images to confirm they are clear and do not need to be repeated.

What Does It Feel Like?

  • A mammogram takes only a few moments with minimal discomfort
  • You may feel pressure during compression
  • Sensitivity can be higher right before or during your period

Tips for Getting a Mammogram

  • Try not to schedule the week before or during your period if you tend to be tender
  • Don’t wear deodorant, perfume, or powder the day of your exam
  • Consider wearing a top with pants or a skirt (you will undress from the waist up)

When Will I Get My Results?

A radiologist reads your mammogram and reports the results to you and your doctor.

If Your Mammogram Is Normal

Continue to get mammograms according to recommended time intervals. Mammograms work best when they can be compared with previous exams to look for changes.

If Your Mammogram Is Abnormal

An abnormal mammogram does not always mean cancer. You may need additional mammograms, tests, or exams before a doctor can tell for sure. You may also be referred to a breast specialist or a surgeon.

If You Haven’t Received Results

Contact your health care provider or the mammography facility if you do not receive a report of your results within 30 days.
