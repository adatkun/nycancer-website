Fluid Drainage

What is Paracentesis?

Paracentesis is a minimally invasive procedure that removes excess fluid that has built up in your abdomen (belly). This fluid accumulation, called ascites, can cause significant discomfort, pain, and difficulty breathing.

By draining this fluid, paracentesis provides quick relief from these symptoms and can help your doctors better understand what's causing the fluid buildup. The removed fluid is sometimes sent to a laboratory for analysis.

Benefits of Paracentesis:

  • Relieves abdominal pain and pressure
  • Improves breathing by reducing pressure on the diaphragm
  • Allows fluid analysis for diagnostic purposes

During Your Procedure

A quick, comfortable process for symptom relief

1

Ultrasound Localization

Your interventional radiologist uses ultrasound to identify the area in your abdomen with the most fluid accumulation.

2

Site Preparation

The selected area is thoroughly cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic (lidocaine/novocaine) for your comfort.

3

Fluid Access

A needle or small catheter (plastic tube) is inserted into the fluid. For larger amounts, a slightly larger catheter may be placed over a wire—but even our largest catheter is skinnier than a pencil.

4

Drainage

The excess fluid is gently drained from your abdomen. The time required depends on the amount of fluid present.

5

Completion

Once enough fluid has been removed, the catheter is withdrawn and the puncture site is covered with skin glue (Dermabond®) or a small bandage.

After Your Procedure

Recovery and site care instructions

Recovery

  • You'll rest in our recovery area for a short time after the procedure
  • Tell your doctor or nurse if you feel dizzy or lightheaded
  • Some pain or discomfort is normal
  • Over-the-counter medications like Tylenol® or Advil® can help if needed

Caring for Your Puncture Site

  • Do not get the area wet for 24 hours
  • After 24 hours, you may shower but continue avoiding getting the site wet
  • No baths or swimming for at least 3 days

What's Normal After Your Procedure

It can sometimes be normal for a small amount of fluid to leak from the puncture site after your procedure. Your doctor or nurse will provide you with bandages to protect your clothing in case this happens.

Important: If fluid is still leaking from the puncture site 24 hours after your procedure, call your doctor or nurse.

When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your healthcare provider if you experience:

Dizziness or Lightheadedness — that doesn't improve
Continued Fluid Leakage — beyond 24 hours
Increasing Pain — not relieved by medication
