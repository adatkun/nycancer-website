Thermal Ablation

Targeted tumor destruction using precise microwave energy

Tumor Treatment

What is Thermal Ablation?

Thermal ablation is a minimally invasive procedure that uses heat to destroy tumors. During the procedure, your interventional radiologist inserts a specialized probe through the skin directly into the target tumor, guided by CT (cat scan) or ultrasound imaging for precise placement.

Once the probe is positioned within the tumor, it emits microwave energy that generates heat, effectively "burning" and destroying the cancerous tissue. Your interventional radiologist carefully controls both the temperature and duration of heating to maximize tumor destruction while protecting surrounding healthy tissue.

In some cases, a biopsy may also be performed during the same procedure, allowing tissue samples to be collected for analysis.

Before Your Procedure

Preparing for your thermal ablation

IV Line Placement

An IV line will be placed in your arm to deliver fluids and medications during the procedure.

Sedation or Anesthesia

You may receive medication that will make you drowsy or may put you to sleep, controlling pain and anxiety throughout.

During Your Procedure

What happens during thermal ablation

1

Imaging & Planning

A CT scan or ultrasound is performed to visualize the tumor. Your interventional radiologist marks the entry point on your skin with a washable marker.

2

Site Preparation

The marked area is thoroughly cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic (lidocaine/novocaine). There are no incisions—only a small probe entry point.

3

Probe Placement

Using real-time image guidance, the ablation probe is carefully inserted through the skin and positioned within the target tumor.

4

Thermal Treatment

The probe emits microwave energy, generating precise heat to destroy the tumor. Temperature and timing are carefully controlled to maximize tumor destruction while protecting surrounding healthy tissue.

5

Biopsy (If Needed)

If required, tissue samples may be collected during the same procedure for pathological analysis.

6

Completion

The probe is removed and the site is covered with skin glue (Dermabond®) or a small bandage.

After Your Procedure

Recovery and site care

Recovery Observation

  • You'll stay in our recovery area for 2-3 hours
  • Our team monitors you to ensure you're fully awake and feeling well
  • Some discomfort in the ablation area is normal
  • Discomfort typically improves within 3-5 days and may be relieved with medication

Caring for Your Access Site

  • Do not get the area wet for 24 hours
  • After 24 hours, you may shower but continue avoiding getting the site wet
  • No baths or swimming for at least 1 week

Dermabond Note: Don't pick or scratch the skin glue—it will naturally come off on its own.

Activity Restrictions

For the first 5 days after your procedure:

No Heavy Lifting Don't lift anything heavier than 10 pounds
No Rigorous Exercise Avoid strenuous physical activity
Light Activities OK Resume usual light activities after 24 hours

When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your healthcare provider immediately if you experience:

Fever Temperature of 100.4°F (38.3°C) or higher
Worsening Symptoms Uncontrolled pain, nausea, or vomiting that's worse than before treatment
Access Site Changes Redness, swelling, or pain around where the probe was placed
