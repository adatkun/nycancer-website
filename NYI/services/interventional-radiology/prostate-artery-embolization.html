Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE)

A minimally invasive alternative to surgery for treating enlarged prostate (BPH)

BPH Treatment

What is Prostate Artery Embolization?

Prostate Artery Embolization (PAE) is a minimally invasive procedure that treats benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)—an enlarged prostate—by reducing blood flow to the prostate gland. This causes the prostate to shrink, relieving the urinary symptoms that affect quality of life.

During PAE, your interventional radiologist threads a small catheter through an artery in your groin or wrist to reach the prostatic arteries. Tiny particles are then injected to block blood flow, causing the prostate to gradually decrease in size.

Proven Benefits of PAE:

  • Reduces prostate size
  • Improves urinary flow
  • Improves overall quality of life

PAE vs. TURP Surgery

Studies have shown that PAE has similar effectiveness to TURP (transurethral resection of the prostate), the traditional surgical treatment for BPH. However, PAE offers a significant advantage: fewer side effects than surgery. This makes PAE an attractive option for men seeking relief from BPH symptoms without the risks associated with surgical intervention.

Before Your Procedure

Several tests ensure PAE is the right treatment for you

Urodynamic Testing

Tests at your urologist's office evaluate your urinary function and flow.

PSA Levels

Blood test to check prostate-specific antigen levels.

MRI Scan

Imaging to visualize your prostate and plan the procedure.

Urinary Catheter

Placed by your urologist prior to your PAE procedure.

These tests ensure there is nothing else causing your symptoms besides BPH.

On the Day of Your Procedure

IV Line Placement

An IV will be placed in your arm for fluids and medications.

Sedation for Comfort

Medication will help you feel relaxed and drowsy, controlling pain and anxiety.

During Your Procedure

What happens during Prostate Artery Embolization

1

Access Point Selection

Using ultrasound guidance, your interventional radiologist selects an artery in your groin or wrist as the access point.

2

Site Preparation

The area is thoroughly cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic (lidocaine/novocaine).

3

Catheter Navigation

Using fluoroscopy (video x-ray), the interventional radiologist guides the catheter to both the left and right prostatic arteries.

4

Position Confirmation

Contrast dye is used to confirm the catheter is positioned correctly in each prostatic artery.

5

Embolization

Tiny particles are injected to block blood flow to the prostate until adequate reduction is achieved on both sides.

6

Completion

The catheter is removed and the access site is covered with a sterile dressing.

After Your Procedure

Recovery begins in our care

Recovery Observation

  • You'll stay in our recovery area for 2-4 hours
  • Our team monitors you to ensure you're fully awake and feeling well

Groin Access: If we accessed through your groin, you'll need to lie down and keep that leg straight during recovery.

Caring for Your Access Site

  • Do not get the area wet for 24 hours
  • After 24 hours, you may shower but avoid getting the site wet
  • No baths or swimming for at least 7 days

Recovery at Home

Understanding post-embolization syndrome

Post-Embolization Syndrome

It's common to experience some symptoms after PAE as your body responds to the treatment. These typically improve within 3-5 days:

Nausea and/or vomiting Can often be managed with medication
Pain at treatment site Most common side effect as blood supply is reduced
Blood in urine and/or sperm Normal during initial recovery
Pain with urination/ejaculation Should improve as healing progresses
Bladder spasms Temporary and typically resolve quickly
Low-grade fever & fatigue May last up to 1 week

Contact your doctor if these symptoms persist beyond 10 days.

Activity Restrictions

For the first 5 days after your procedure:

No Heavy Lifting Don't lift anything heavier than 10 pounds
No Rigorous Exercise Avoid strenuous physical activity
Light Activities OK Resume usual light activities after 24 hours

When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your healthcare provider immediately if you experience:

Fever Temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher
Uncontrolled Symptoms Pain, nausea, or vomiting not relieved by medication
Access Site Changes Redness, swelling, or bleeding around the site
Any Concerning Symptoms Anything that worries you about your recovery
