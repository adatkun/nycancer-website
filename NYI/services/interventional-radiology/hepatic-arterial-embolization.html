Hepatic Arterial Embolization

Minimally invasive treatment targeting liver tumors through precision catheter techniques

Precision Treatment

What is Hepatic Arterial Embolization?

Hepatic embolization is a minimally invasive procedure that blocks blood flow to liver tumors, helping to eliminate cancerous cells while preserving healthy liver tissue.

During the procedure, your interventional radiologist threads a small, flexible catheter into your hepatic artery. Through this catheter, tiny particles are injected that block blood flow to the tumor, effectively starving it of the nutrients it needs to survive.

In some cases, we may also inject chemotherapy drugs or radioactive particles (Y-90) directly into the tumor for enhanced treatment effectiveness. This targeted approach delivers treatment precisely where it's needed while minimizing impact on surrounding healthy tissue.

Before Your Procedure

What to expect as you prepare for your hepatic embolization

IV Line Placement

An IV line will be placed in your arm to deliver fluids and medications throughout your procedure.

Sedation Medication

You may receive medication through your IV that will help you feel relaxed and drowsy, controlling pain and anxiety.

Urinary Catheter

Depending on your specific case, a urinary catheter may be placed at the beginning of the procedure.

During Your Procedure

A step-by-step look at the hepatic embolization process

1

Access Point Selection

Using ultrasound guidance, your interventional radiologist will select an artery in your groin or wrist as the access point for the procedure.

2

Site Preparation

The selected area will be thoroughly cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic (lidocaine/novocaine) to ensure your comfort.

3

Catheter Navigation

Using fluoroscopy (video x-ray), the interventional radiologist guides the catheter into your hepatic artery with precision accuracy.

4

Position Confirmation

A contrast dye is used to confirm the catheter is positioned correctly within the blood vessel supplying the tumor.

5

Embolization Treatment

Tiny particles, chemotherapy agents, or radioactive particles (Y-90) are injected until blood flow to the tumor is adequately blocked.

6

Completion

The catheter is carefully removed and the access site is covered with a sterile dressing.

After Your Procedure

Your recovery begins in our care

Recovery Area Monitoring

You will stay in our comfortable recovery area for 2 to 4 hours following your procedure. During this time, our medical team will monitor you to ensure you are fully awake and feeling well.

Groin Access Note

If the procedure was performed through an artery in your groin, you will need to lie down and keep that leg straight while in the recovery area. This helps ensure proper healing of the access site.

Recovery at Home

Important information for your continued recovery

Post-Embolization Syndrome

It's normal to experience some symptoms after your procedure. These typically improve within 3-5 days:

  • Nausea and/or vomiting — can often be managed with medication
  • Pain at treatment site — the most common side effect, occurs as blood supply is cut off
  • Low-grade fever — may last up to 1 week
  • Lethargy and fatigue — typically resolves within 1 week

Contact your doctor if symptoms persist beyond 10 days.

Access Site Care

  • Keep the access site dry for the first 24 hours
  • After 24 hours, you may shower but avoid getting the site wet
  • No baths or swimming for at least 7 days

Activity Restrictions

  • No lifting over 10 pounds for 5 days
  • No rigorous exercise for 5 days
  • Light activities may resume after 24 hours

Special Considerations for Y-90 (Radioembolization)

If you received radioactive particles during your procedure, please follow these additional precautions:

  • Do not attempt to become pregnant or get someone pregnant
  • Nursing mothers should not nurse following this procedure
  • Sit to urinate for the first 24-48 hours and flush twice after each use

When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your healthcare provider immediately if you experience any of the following:

Fever Temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher
Uncontrolled Symptoms Pain, nausea, or vomiting that doesn't improve with medication
Access Site Changes Redness, swelling, or bleeding around the procedure site
Any Concerning Symptoms Anything that worries you about your recovery
