Percutaneous Needle Biopsy

Precise, image-guided tissue sampling using CT or ultrasound technology

Image-Guided Precision

What is a Percutaneous Needle Biopsy?

A percutaneous needle biopsy is a minimally invasive procedure that allows your interventional radiologist to collect a tissue sample by inserting a needle through your skin. Using advanced imaging technology—either CT (cat scan) or ultrasound—your doctor can precisely guide the needle to the area of concern.

Before your procedure, your interventional radiologist carefully reviews your scans to determine the safest and most effective path for obtaining your tissue sample. This planning ensures accuracy while minimizing any impact on surrounding tissues.

The collected tissue is examined by a pathologist, who analyzes the cells to help diagnose your condition. This procedure provides valuable diagnostic information without the need for surgical incisions.

Before Your Procedure

Preparing for your image-guided biopsy

IV Line Placement

An IV line will be placed in your arm to deliver fluids and medications during the procedure.

Sedation for Comfort

You may receive medication through your IV that will help you feel relaxed and drowsy, controlling any pain and anxiety.

During Your Procedure

What happens during your needle biopsy

1

Imaging & Planning

A CT scan or ultrasound is performed in the area of concern. Your interventional radiologist uses this imaging to plan the optimal needle path.

2

Site Marking

The interventional radiologist draws a mark on your skin with a washable marker to identify the precise entry point.

3

Site Preparation

The marked area is thoroughly cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic (lidocaine/novocaine) for your comfort.

4

Tissue Sampling

Using real-time image guidance, the needle is carefully inserted to the target area and tissue samples are collected. There are no incisions—only a small needle hole.

5

Sample Verification

A pathologist immediately examines the tissue slides. If adequate tissue has been obtained, the procedure is complete. If needed, 1-2 additional samples may be taken.

6

Completion

The needle is removed and the site is covered with skin glue (Dermabond®) or a small bandage.

After Your Procedure

Recovery and caring for your biopsy site

Recovery Observation

  • You'll stay in our recovery area for 30 minutes to 3 hours
  • Recovery time varies based on the biopsy location and your individual needs
  • Some discomfort at the needle site is normal and typically improves within 24-48 hours
  • Over-the-counter pain medication can help if needed; most patients don't require any

Caring for Your Biopsy Site

  • Do not get the area wet for 24 hours
  • After 24 hours, you may shower but continue avoiding getting the site wet
  • No baths or swimming for at least 1 week

Dermabond Note: Don't pick or scratch the skin glue—it will naturally come off on its own.

Activity Restrictions

For the first 5 days after your biopsy:

No Heavy Lifting Don't lift anything heavier than 10 pounds
No Rigorous Exercise Avoid strenuous physical activity
Light Activities OK Resume usual light activities after 24 hours

When to Call Your Interventional Radiologist

Contact us immediately if you experience any of the following:

Increased Pain New or worsening pain at the biopsy site
Swelling or Growing Bruise At the biopsy site
Pus or Fluid Drainage Coming from your biopsy site
Signs of Infection Site is hot, tender, red, or irritated
Fever Temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) or higher
Chills Unexplained shaking or feeling cold
