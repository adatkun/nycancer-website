Mediport (Port) Placement

Long-term IV access for easier medication delivery and blood draws

Long-Term Access

What is a Mediport?

A Mediport (also called an implanted port) is a small medical device placed under your skin in your chest, approximately 1 inch below your collarbone. About the size of a nickel or quarter, the port has a flexible tube (catheter) that enters into a vein in your neck or chest.

Ports can remain in place for as long as you need them, providing reliable long-term access to your bloodstream. This makes it much easier for your healthcare team to deliver treatments and monitor your health.

Your Port Makes It Easier To:

  • Receive intravenous (IV) chemotherapy, fluids, and other medications
  • Have blood samples taken without repeated needle sticks

Accessing Your Port

When it's time for treatment, your nurse will place a needle through the access point on your port. Medication or fluid flows through the port, into the catheter, and directly into your bloodstream—no need to search for a vein each time.

Before Your Procedure

Port placement is a short procedure with minimal preparation needed

IV Line Placement

An IV line will be placed in your arm to deliver fluids and medications during the procedure.

Sedation for Comfort

You may receive medication through your IV that will help you feel relaxed and drowsy, controlling any pain and anxiety.

During Your Procedure

What happens during Mediport placement

1

Site Preparation

The area where your port will be placed is thoroughly cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic (lidocaine/novocaine).

2

Vein Access

Your interventional radiologist accesses a vein at the base of your neck using image guidance for precision.

3

Incision & Tunneling

A small incision is made on your chest below your collarbone. The catheter is placed through the incision, tunneled under your skin, and threaded into the vein.

4

Port Placement

The port is positioned in the pocket created under your skin, connected to the catheter, and secured in place.

5

Closure

Your incisions are closed with absorbable sutures (no removal needed) or surgical glue (Dermabond®).

After Your Procedure

Recovery and caring for your new port

Recovery Observation

  • You'll stay in our recovery area for approximately 1 hour
  • Our team monitors you to ensure you're fully awake and feeling well
  • Some discomfort at the incision sites and where the catheter was tunneled is normal
  • Discomfort typically improves within 24-48 hours
  • Over-the-counter pain medication can help if needed; most patients don't require any

Caring for Your Port Site

  • Do not get the area wet for 24 hours
  • After 24 hours, you may shower but continue avoiding getting the site wet
  • No baths or swimming for at least 1 week

Dermabond Note: Don't pick or scratch the surgical glue—it will naturally come off on its own.

Activity Restrictions

For the first 5 days after your port placement:

No Heavy Lifting Don't lift anything heavier than 10 pounds
No Rigorous Exercise Avoid strenuous physical activity
Light Activities OK Usual light activities may resume after 24 hours
Seatbelt Tip Place a small pillow or towel between the strap and your body for comfort

When to Call Your Doctor

Call Your Interventional Radiologist

For concerns about your port site:

  • New or increased pain at the port site
  • Swelling or a growing bruise at the port site
  • Pus or fluid coming from your incision(s)
  • Incision(s) are hot, tender, red, or irritated

Call Your Healthcare Provider

For signs of infection:

Fever — 100.4°F (38°C) or higher
Chills — unexplained shaking or feeling cold
