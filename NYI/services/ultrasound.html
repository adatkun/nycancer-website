What is Ultrasound?

Ultrasound is one of the most popular and versatile medical imaging technologies. Using high-frequency sound waves imperceptible to human ears, ultrasound produces real-time images of internal organs and soft-tissue structures including blood vessels and muscles.

This non-invasive, painless imaging technology is used to determine causes of pain, infection, and swelling in internal organs. It also aids in prenatal examinations, guides biopsies, diagnoses heart conditions, and assesses damage after a heart attack.

Unlike X-rays or CT scans, ultrasound does not expose patients to any radiation, making it one of the safest imaging options available.

Sound Wave Technology

How Ultrasound Works

Ultrasound operates under the same principles originally developed to track submarines, allowing for non-invasive imaging without any radiation exposure.

A small device called a transducer is placed on the skin and emits high-frequency sound waves that pulse through your body. These pulses "echo" back and are received by the transducer, which sends the information to a computer for analysis.

The computer converts this data into real-time images displayed on a monitor, allowing technologists to examine blood flow, determine the characteristics of organs and tissues, and detect abnormal masses like tumors.

Common Uses for Ultrasound

Ultrasound is a versatile diagnostic tool used to evaluate many conditions throughout the body.

Abdominal Imaging

Evaluates liver, gallbladder, pancreas, spleen, and kidneys for disease or abnormalities.

Prenatal Exams

Monitors fetal development and health throughout pregnancy.

Heart Conditions

Diagnoses heart conditions and assesses damage after a heart attack.

Blood Flow

Doppler ultrasound evaluates blood flow through vessels and arteries.

Biopsy Guidance

Provides real-time imaging to guide needle placement during biopsies.

Mass Detection

Identifies and characterizes abnormal masses, cysts, and tumors.

What to Expect

Ultrasound is a painless, comfortable procedure that typically takes 15-20 minutes to complete.

1

Preparation

You'll wear comfortable, loose-fitting clothing or a hospital gown depending on the area being examined.

2

Positioning

Lie face up on a padded examination table. You may be asked to turn to either side to enhance image quality.

3

The Scan

Warm, water-based gel is applied to your skin. The technologist moves a transducer over the area, capturing real-time images.

4

After the Exam

The gel is wiped off and you can resume normal activities immediately. No recovery time needed.

About the Gel

The warm, water-based gel applied during your exam prevents air from getting between your skin and the transducer, allowing sound waves to travel effectively into your body. It wipes off easily after the procedure.

Preparing for Your Ultrasound

Preparation varies by exam type. Most ultrasounds require no special preparation, but some require specific steps.

Abdominal Ultrasound

  • Fast for at least 8 hours before exam
  • No food or liquids
  • Food and liquid in the stomach can obscure images

Pelvic Ultrasound

  • Full bladder required
  • Drink 24 oz of water 1 hour before exam
  • No fasting or sedation needed

Renal Ultrasound

  • Full bladder required
  • Drink 24 oz of water 1 hour before exam
  • No fasting or sedation needed

No Preparation Required

The following ultrasound types do not require any special patient preparation: Thyroid, Venous Doppler, Carotid, Scrotal, and Soft Tissue ultrasounds. You can continue with your usual medications for all ultrasound exams.
